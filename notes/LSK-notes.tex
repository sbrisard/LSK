\begin{abstract}
  These are my notes on the LSK method for the analysis of the stability and
  bifurcation(s) of a conservative system. These notes are based on several
  references: Koiter's initial PhD thesis~\parencite{koit1945} as well as some
  graphical illustrations from his lecture notes~\parencite{koit2009}. I enjoyed
  the concise presentation of \textcite{nguy2000} as well as the lecture notes
  of \textcite{tria2017}. Finally, the chapter by \textcite{poti1987} helped me
  clear some issues.

  These notes by S√©bastien Brisard are licensed under a Creative Commons
  Attribution 4.0 International License. To view a copy of this license, visit
  \url{http://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/4.0/}.

  I hope the reader will find these notes useful, even though there are still a
  few points which I do not fully understand (they are clearly indicated in the
  text).
\end{abstract}

\section{Analysis of the critical point}
\label{sec:20220802061621}

In this section, we discuss the stability of the critical point \((u‚ÇÄ, Œª‚ÇÄ)\). To
this end, we evaluate the potential energy in a neighboring state
\(u‚ÇÄ + u\), where \(u ‚àà U\) is ``small''. We have, to the fourth order
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}[b]
    ‚Ñ∞(u‚ÇÄ + u, Œª‚ÇÄ) - ‚Ñ∞(u‚ÇÄ, Œª‚ÇÄ) ={}
    &\tfrac{1}{2} ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; u, u) + \tfrac{1}{6} ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ; u, u, u)\\
    &+ \tfrac{1}{24} ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(Œª‚ÇÄ; u, u, u, u) + o(‚å© u , u ‚å™¬≤),
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where the linear term has been omitted, \(u‚ÇÄ\) being a critical point of the
energy. Since \(v ‚àà V\), we have \(‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, ‚Ä¢) = 0\). We now expand \(u\) as
\(u = Œæ v + Œ∑ w\), with \(Œæ, Œ∑ ‚àà \reals\) and \(v ‚àà V\) and \(w ‚àà W\) are fixed,
orthogonal directions. Owing to the multi-linearity and symmetry of the
successive differential of \(‚Ñ∞\), the above expression expands as
follows
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}[b]
    ‚Ñ∞(u‚ÇÄ + u, Œª‚ÇÄ) - ‚Ñ∞(u‚ÇÄ, Œª‚ÇÄ) ={}
    & \tfrac{1}{2} Œ∑¬≤ ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; w, w) + \tfrac{1}{6} Œæ¬≥ ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, v, v)\\
    & + \tfrac{1}{2} Œæ¬≤ Œ∑ ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, v, w) + \tfrac{1}{2} Œæ Œ∑¬≤ ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, w, w)\\
    & + \tfrac{1}{6} Œ∑¬≥ ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ; w, w, w) + \tfrac{1}{24} Œæ‚Å¥ ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, v, v, v)\\
    & + \tfrac{1}{6} Œæ¬≥ Œ∑ ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, v, v, w) + \tfrac{1}{4} Œæ¬≤ Œ∑¬≤ ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, v, w, w)\\
    & + \tfrac{1}{6} Œæ Œ∑¬≥ ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, w, w, w) + \tfrac{1}{24} Œ∑‚Å¥ ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(Œª‚ÇÄ; w, w, w, w)\\
    & + o\bigl[\bigl(Œæ¬≤ + Œ∑¬≤\bigr)¬≤\bigr].
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

For the equilibrium to be stable, the above expression must be \(‚â• 0\) for all
\(Œæ\) et \(Œ∑\) small enough. Taking first \(Œ∑ = 0\), we get the following necessary conditions
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20211108164416}
  ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, v, v) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, v, v, v) \geq 0 \quad \text{for all} \quad v‚ààV.
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}
  Note that, from Theorem~\ref{thr:20220802112835}, the first of these two
  conditions is equivalent to \(E_{ijk}=0\), for all \(i, j, k = 1, \ldots m\).
\end{remark}

In other words, if there exists \(v ‚àà V\) such that \(‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, v, v) \neq 0\)
or \(‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(v, v, v, v) < 0\), then the equilibrium is \emph{unstable} at the
critical point. The above conditions are not sufficient. Indeed, assuming
conditions~\eqref{eq:20211108164416} to hold, we now take \(Œ∑ = Œæ¬≤\)
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}[b]
    ‚Ñ∞(u‚ÇÄ + u, Œª‚ÇÄ) - ‚Ñ∞(u‚ÇÄ, Œª‚ÇÄ) ={} & \tfrac{1}{2} Œæ‚Å¥ \bigl[ ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; w, w) + ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, v, w)\\
    & + \tfrac{1}{12} ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, v, v, v) \bigr] + o(Œæ‚Å¥)
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
and we get the further necessary condition
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20211109145356}
  ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(w, w) + ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(v, v, w) + \tfrac{1}{12} ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(v, v, v, v) \geq 0 \quad \text{for all} \quad v ‚àà V \quad \text{and} \quad w ‚àà W.
\end{equation}

The direction \(v‚ààV\) being fixed, the above expression is minimal when \(w\)
satisfies the following variational problem
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20211109145224}
  2‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(w, \hat{w}) +‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(v, v, \hat{w}) = 0 \quad \text{for all} \quad \hat{w}‚ààW.
\end{equation}

Expanding \(v ‚àà V\) in the \((v_i)\) basis, it is observed that the solution to
the above variational problem is \(w = \tfrac{1}{2} Œæ^i Œæ^j w_{ij}\), where
\(w_{ij}\) is the solution to the elementary variational problem
\eqref{eq:20220519164523}. For this value of \(w\),
condition~\eqref{eq:20211109145356} reads
\begin{equation}
  \bigl[‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(v_i, v_j, v_k, v_l) - 3‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(w_{ij}, w_{kl})\bigr] Œæ^i Œæ^j Œæ^k Œæ^l \geq 0 \quad \text{for all} \quad Œæ¬π, \ldots, Œæ^m ‚àà \reals,
\end{equation}
which, in view of definition~\eqref{eq:20220802081116} of \(E_{ijkl}\), is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
  E_{ijkl} Œæ^i Œæ^j Œæ^k Œæ^l \geq 0 \quad \text{for all} \quad Œæ¬π, \ldots, Œæ^m ‚àà \reals.
\end{equation}

Note that Eq.~\eqref{eq:20211109145224} implies \(‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v, v, v, v) ‚â• 0\),
which becomes a redundant necessary condition. Indeed, plugging
\(w= Œæ^i Œæ^j w_{ij}\) into Eq.~\eqref{eq:20211109145224} cancels the first two
terms. To sum up, we have the following \emph{necessary} conditions for
stability
\begin{equation}
  E_{ijk} Œæ^i Œæ^j Œæ^k = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad E_{ijkl} Œæ^i Œæ^j Œæ^k Œæ^l ‚â• 0 \quad \text{for all} \quad Œæ¬π, \ldots, Œæ^m ‚àà \reals.
\end{equation}

\pdfmarkupcomment{Conversely, the following condition is \emph{sufficient} to ensure stability of the critical point}{\`A d\'emontrer}
\begin{equation}
  E_{ijk} Œæ^i Œæ^j Œæ^k = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad E_{ijkl} Œæ^i Œæ^j Œæ^k Œæ^l > 0 \quad \text{for all} \quad Œæ¬π, \ldots, Œæ^m ‚àà \reals.
\end{equation}

\section{Analysis of bifurcated branches}
\label{sec:20220617075558}

In this section, we show that, besides the fundamental branch \(u^\ast(Œª)\),
other (bifurcated) equilibrium branches may pass through the critical point
\((u‚ÇÄ, Œª‚ÇÄ)\). The starting point is the characterization of an equilibrium by
the stationarity of the energy. Following the LSK method, the equilibrium state
\(u ‚àà U\) is decomposed as follows
\begin{equation}
  u = u^\ast(Œª) + Œæ^i v_i + w, \quad \text{with} \quad w ‚àà W.
\end{equation}

It follows from the orthogonality of \(V\) and \(W\) that \(‚å©v_i, w‚å™ = 0\) for
all \(i=1, \ldots, m\). Stationarity of the energy is expressed as follows
\begin{equation}
  ‚Ñ∞_{,u}[u^\ast(Œª) + Œæ^i v_i + w, Œª; \hat{u}] = 0 \quad\text{for all }\hat{u} ‚àà U
\end{equation}
or, equivalently
\begin{equation}
  ‚Ñ∞_{,u}[u^\ast(Œª) + Œæ^i v_i + w, Œª; \hat{v}] = 0 \quad\text{for all }\hat{v} ‚àà V,\\
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220825143616}
  ‚Ñ∞_{,u}[u^\ast(Œª) + Œæ^i v_i + w, Œª; \hat{w}] = 0 \quad\text{for all }\hat{w} ‚àà W.
\end{equation}

From the implicit function theorem, Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220825143616} defines a
function \((Œæ^i, Œª) \mapsto w(Œæ^i, Œª)\) in the neighborhood of
\((Œæ^1, \ldots, Œæ^m, Œª) = (0, \ldots, 0, Œª‚ÇÄ)\). Why the theorem applies will be
clearer below. Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220825143616} is first differentiated \wrt{}
\(Œæ^i\)
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220826140926}
  ‚Ñ∞_{,uu}(u^\ast + Œæ^k v_k + w, Œª; v_i + w_{,i}, \hat{w}) = 0\quad\text{for all }\hat{w} ‚àà W.
\end{equation}

Substituting \(Œæ^1 = \cdots = Œæ^m = 0, Œª = Œª‚ÇÄ\) in the above equations and
observing that \(‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v_i, \hat{w}) = 0\) since \(v_i ‚àà V\), we get
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:20220825150219}

  \label{eq:20220601070917}
  \tfrac{1}{3} E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l + Œª‚ÇÅ \bigl( \mathring{E}_{ijk} Œæ‚ÇÅ^k + Œª‚ÇÅ \mathring{F}_{ij} \bigr)Œæ‚ÇÅ^j + \bigl(E_{ijk} Œæ‚ÇÅ^k + Œª‚ÇÅ F_{ij}\bigr) Œæ‚ÇÇ^j + Œª‚ÇÇ F_{ij} Œæ‚ÇÅ^j = 0.
\end{equation}

In order to analyse the stability of the bifurcated branches thus found, one
must look at the Hessian of the energy. It is first observed that, on the
fundamental branch
\begin{equation}
 ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(Œª; \hat{u}, \hat{v}) = ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; \hat{u}, \hat{v}) + \bigl(Œª - Œª‚ÇÄ\bigr) \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; \hat{u}, \hat{v}) + o(Œª - Œª‚ÇÄ).
\end{equation}

In what follows, it will be assumed that \(\dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ)‚â†0\) and that \(‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(Œª)\)
(which is positive definite over \(V\) for \(Œª<Œª‚ÇÄ\) and null for \(Œª=Œª‚ÇÄ\)) is
negative definite for \(Œª>Œª‚ÇÄ\) sufficiently small (the fundamental branch is
strictly unstable beyond the critical load). From the above expansion, it
results that \(\dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ)\) is negative definite over \(V\). In other words,
\(-F_{ij}\) is a positive definite tensor. The asymptotic expansion of the
Hessian of the energy along the bifurcated branch is derived in
appendix~\ref{sec:20220616055207}. For all \(\hat{u}, \hat{v}‚ààU\)
\begin{multline}
  \label{eq:20220531054247}
  ‚Ñ∞_{, uu}[u(Œ∑), Œª(Œ∑); \hat{u}, \hat{v}] = ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ ; \hat{u}, \hat{v}) + Œ∑ \bigl[‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ ; u‚ÇÅ, \hat{u}, \hat{v})  + Œª‚ÇÅ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; \hat{u}, \hat{v})\bigr]\\
  + \tfrac{1}{2} Œ∑¬≤ \bigl[‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(Œª‚ÇÄ; u‚ÇÅ, u‚ÇÅ, \hat{u}, \hat{v}) + ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ; u‚ÇÇ, \hat{u}, \hat{v}) + 2Œª‚ÇÅ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ; u‚ÇÅ, \hat{u}, \hat{v})\\
  + Œª‚ÇÅ¬≤ \ddot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; \hat{u}, \hat{v}) + Œª‚ÇÇ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; \hat{u}, \hat{v}) \bigr] + o(Œ∑¬≤).
\end{multline}

Stability analysis is performed by means of the eigenvalues \(Œ± ‚àà \reals\) and
eigenvectors \(x ‚àà U\) of the Hessian
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220617074949}
  ‚Ñ∞_{, u u} [u(Œ∑), Œª(Œ∑); x, \hat{u}] = Œ± ‚å© x, \hat{u} ‚å™ \quad \text{for all} \quad \hat{u} ‚àà V,
\end{equation}
where \(Œ±\) and \(x\) are expanded to second order in \(Œ∑\)
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220617064633}
  Œ± = Œ±‚ÇÄ + Œ∑ Œ±‚ÇÅ + \tfrac{1}{2} Œ∑¬≤ Œ±‚ÇÇ + o(Œ∑¬≤)
  \quad \text{and} \quad
  x = x‚ÇÄ + Œ∑ x‚ÇÅ + \tfrac{1}{2} Œ∑¬≤ x‚ÇÇ + o(Œ∑¬≤).
\end{equation}

The following results are proved in Appendix~\ref{sec:20220616074108}: first,
\((Œ±‚ÇÄ, x_0)\) is necessarily an eigenpair of \(‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ)\). Since \(‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ (Œª‚ÇÄ)\) is
positive, \(Œ±‚ÇÄ ‚â• 0\). If \(Œ±‚ÇÄ>0\), then \(Œ±>0\) in the neighborhood of
\(Œª‚ÇÄ\). Potentially unstable modes are therefore such that \(Œ±‚ÇÄ=0\). In other
words, \(x‚ÇÄ ‚àà V\); furthermore, \((Œ±‚ÇÅ, œá‚ÇÄ^i)\) is an eigenpair of the symmetric
tensor \((E_{ijk} Œæ‚ÇÅ^k + Œª‚ÇÅ F_{ij})\)
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220609133608}
  x‚ÇÄ = œá‚ÇÄ^i v_i
  \quad \text{and} \quad
  \bigl(E_{ijk} Œæ‚ÇÅ^k + Œª‚ÇÅ F_{ij} \bigr) œá‚ÇÄ^j = Œ±‚ÇÅ œá‚ÇÄ^i.
\end{equation}
As for the higher order terms, it is also found that
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220609133629}
  x‚ÇÅ = œá‚ÇÅ^i v_i +  œá‚ÇÄ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j w_{i j} + \tfrac{1}{2} Œª‚ÇÅ œá‚ÇÄ^i w_i
\end{equation}
and
\begin{multline}
  \label{eq:20220616082923}
  \bigl[E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l + Œª‚ÇÅ\bigl(2 \mathring{E}_{ijk} Œæ‚ÇÅ^k + Œª‚ÇÅ \mathring{F}_{ij}\bigr) + E_{ijk} Œæ‚ÇÇ^k + Œª‚ÇÇ F_{ij} \bigr] œá‚ÇÄ^j\\
  + 2\bigl(E_{ijk}  Œæ‚ÇÅ^k + Œª‚ÇÅ F_{ij} \bigr) œá‚ÇÅ^j = 2Œ±‚ÇÅœá‚ÇÅ^i + Œ±‚ÇÇ œá‚ÇÄ^i.
\end{multline}

Finally, to close this analysis of the bifurcated branches, the following
asymptotic expansion of the energy is derived in
Appendix~\ref{sec:20220525053434}
\begin{multline}
  \label{eq:20220525053600}
    ‚Ñ∞[u(Œ∑), Œª(Œ∑)] = ‚Ñ∞\{u^{\ast}[Œª(Œ∑)], Œª(Œ∑)\} + \tfrac{1}{6} Œª‚ÇÅ Œ∑¬≥ F_{i j} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j\\
    +\tfrac{1}{24} Œ∑‚Å¥ \bigl[E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l + 4Œª‚ÇÅ \mathring{E}_{ijk} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k + 6 \bigl(Œª‚ÇÅ¬≤ \mathring{F}_{ij} + Œª‚ÇÇ F_{ij}\bigr) Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j\bigr] + o(Œ∑‚Å¥).
\end{multline}

\section{Discussion}

In this section, we discuss the two main cases of bifurcations, namely
\emph{asymmetric} and \emph{symmetric}. In each case, we analyse the stability
of the bifurcated branch.

\begin{remark}
  The boundary case is unclear to me. I think that whether a bifurcation is
  symmetric or asymmetric should depend on the value of \(Œª‚ÇÅ\) only. If
  \(Œª‚ÇÅ ‚â† 0\), the bifurcated branch is \emph{asymmetric}. Conversely, if
  \(Œª‚ÇÅ = 0\) and \(Œª‚ÇÇ ‚â† 0\), then the bifurcated branch is \emph{symmetric}.

  In the literature, the discussion is placed on \(E_{ijk}\). If \(Œª‚ÇÅ ‚â† 0\),
  surely one of the \(E_{ijk}\) is non-zero also. However, I believe it is
  \emph{not} a sufficient condition: one of the bifurcated branches could be
  symmetric \((Œª‚ÇÅ = 0)\), even if all \(E_{ijk}\) are not null. It is true
  however that \emph{all} bifurcated branches are symmetric if, and only if,
  \(E_{ijk}=0\) for all \(i, j, k = 1, \ldots, m\). Therefore, the two cases
  that will be discussed below are: (1) one of the bifurcated branches is
  asymmetric and (2) all bifurcated branches are symmetric. The mixed case ``one
  of the bifurcated branches is symmetric'' will \emph{not} be discussed.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Asymmetric bifurcated branch (\(Œª‚ÇÅ ‚â† 0\))}

We first consider the situation where \(Œª‚ÇÅ ‚â† 0\) on the bifurcated branch. The
bifurcation equation~\eqref{eq:20220524135036} shows that necessarily,
\(E_{ijk}\) is not identically nul. This equation has at most \((2^m - 1)\)
pairs of real solutions \((Œª‚ÇÅ, u‚ÇÅ)\) et \((- Œª‚ÇÅ, - u‚ÇÅ)\); furthermore,
multiplication by \(Œæ_1^i\) shows that
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220801085236}
  Œª‚ÇÅ = -\frac{E_{ijk} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k}{2 F_{ij} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j}.
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}
  I can't prove that the bifurcation equation~\eqref{eq:20220524135036} has at
  most \((2^m - 1)\) pairs of real solutions.
\end{remark}

Along the bifurcated branch, we have \(Œª = Œª‚ÇÄ + Œ∑ Œª‚ÇÅ + o(Œ∑)\), and \(Œ∑\) can be
eliminated. In other words, \(Œ∑=Œª\) (\(Œª‚ÇÅ=1\) and \(Œª‚ÇÇ = Œª‚ÇÉ = \cdots = 0\)) can
be selected as a parameter. It is therefore possible to express the bifurcated
branch as a function of \(Œª\), \(u(Œª)\). For example, combining
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:20220524133816} and \eqref{eq:20220531054247}, we find that
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}[b]
    ‚Ñ∞_{, uu}[u(Œ∑), Œª(Œ∑); u‚ÇÅ, u‚ÇÅ]
    &= Œ∑ \bigl[‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ ; u‚ÇÅ, u‚ÇÅ, u‚ÇÅ)  + Œª‚ÇÅ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; u‚ÇÅ, u‚ÇÅ)\bigr] + o(Œ∑)\\
    &= - Œ∑ Œª‚ÇÅ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; u‚ÇÅ, u‚ÇÅ) + o(Œ∑),
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
or
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220819160235}
  ‚Ñ∞_{, uu}[u(Œª), Œª; u‚ÇÅ, u‚ÇÅ] = -\bigl( Œª - Œª‚ÇÄ \bigr) \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; u‚ÇÅ, u‚ÇÅ) + o(Œª - Œª‚ÇÄ).
\end{equation}

For \(Œª < Œª‚ÇÄ\), the above quantity is \emph{negative} (since \(\dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ\) is
negative definite). In other words

\begin{center}
  \framebox{For asymmetric bifurcations, below the critical load, the bifurcated
    branch is unstable}
\end{center}

To investigate the stability above the critical load, we need to analyse the
sign of the eigenvalues \(Œ±\) of the Hessian. At first order,
\(Œ± = Œ∑ Œ±‚ÇÅ + o(Œ∑)\), where \(Œ±‚ÇÅ\) is an eigenvalue of
\((E_{ijk} Œæ‚ÇÅ^k + Œª‚ÇÅ F_{ij})\). Let \(Œ±_{\min}\) and \(Œ±_{\max}\) be the minimum
and maximum eigenvalues of this second-order tensor. Three cases must be
discussed
\begin{enumerate}
\item If \(Œ±_{\min} Œ±_{\max} > 0\), then \((E_{ijk} Œæ‚ÇÅ^k + Œª‚ÇÅ F_{ij})\) is
  positive or negative definite: all eigenvalues have the same sign,
  \(\epsilon ‚àà \{-1, +1\}\). Then the sign of the eigenvalues \(Œ±\) of the
  Hessian is \(\epsilon Œ∑\) and there is a stability switch at the critical
  load. Since the bifurcated branch is unstable \emph{below} the critical load,
  this means that it is \emph{stable} above the critical load.
\item If \(Œ±_{\min} Œ±_{\max} < 0\), then the extremal eigenvalues of the Hessian
  are \(Œ∑ Œ±_{\min}\) and \(Œ∑ Œ±_{\max}\), the product of which is
  \(Œ∑¬≤ Œ±_{\min} Œ±_{\max} < 0\). The bifurcated branch is \emph{unstable} for all
  values of \(Œª\).
\item If \(Œ±_{\min} Œ±_{\max} = 0\), the analysis is inconclusive.
\end{enumerate}

To close this section, it is observed that the dominant term of the
expansion~\eqref{eq:20220525053600} of the potential energy along the bifurcated
branch is of the third order in \(Œ∑\)
\begin{equation}
  ‚Ñ∞[u(Œ∑), Œª(Œ∑)] = ‚Ñ∞\{u^{\ast}[Œª(Œ∑)], Œª(Œ∑)\} + \tfrac{1}{6} Œª‚ÇÅ Œ∑¬≥ F_{i j} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j + o(Œ∑¬≥).
\end{equation}

Eliminating \(Œª\) and plugging expression~\eqref{eq:20220801085236} of \(Œª‚ÇÅ\)
delivers the expression of the potential energy, where \(Œª\) is the parameter
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}[b]
    ‚Ñ∞[u(Œª), Œª] &= ‚Ñ∞[u^{\ast}(Œª), Œª] + \frac{\bigl(Œª - Œª‚ÇÄ\bigr)¬≥}{6Œª‚ÇÅ¬≤} F_{i j} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j + o(Œª¬≥)\\
    &= ‚Ñ∞[u^{\ast}(Œª), Œª] + \frac{2 \bigl( F_{i j} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j \bigr)¬≥}{3 \bigl( E_{ijk} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k \bigr)¬≤} \bigl(Œª - Œª‚ÇÄ\bigr)¬≥ + o(Œª¬≥).
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Recalling that \(F_{i j} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j < 0\), it is found that, above the critical
load, the potential energy is \emph{smaller} along the bifurcated branch than
along the fundamental branch.

\begin{remark}
  As expected, the above expression does not depend on the scaling of \(u‚ÇÅ\) (of the \(Œæ‚ÇÅ^i\)).
\end{remark}
\begin{remark}
  It has been shown in Sec.~\ref{sec:20220802061621} that, when \(E_{ijk}\) is
  not identically null, the bifurcation point is \emph{unstable}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{A particular case of symmetric bifurcation}

We now consider the case \(E_{ijk}=0\) for all \(i, j, k = 1, \ldots, m\). Then
[see Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220524135036}] \(Œª‚ÇÅ = 0\) on \emph{all} bifurcated
branches. It is assumed that, on the bifurcated branch under consideration, the
next term of the expansion of \(Œª\) is non-zero: \(Œª‚ÇÇ ‚â† 0\). The bifurcation is
\emph{symmetric}, and the bifurcation equation~\eqref{eq:20220601070917} reduces
to
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220801092222}
  \tfrac{1}{3} E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l  + Œª‚ÇÇ F_{ij} Œæ‚ÇÅ^j = 0,
\end{equation}
which has at most \((3^m - 1) / 2\) pairs of real solutions \((Œª‚ÇÇ, u‚ÇÅ)\) and
\((- Œª‚ÇÇ, - u‚ÇÅ)\). Upon multiplication by \(Œæ‚ÇÅ^i\), the above equation delivers
the following expression of \(Œª‚ÇÇ\)
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220801093236}
  Œª‚ÇÇ = -\frac{E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l}{3 F_{ij} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j}.
\end{equation}

Since \(F_{ij} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j < 0\), \(Œª‚ÇÇ\) has the same sign as
\(E_{ijkl}Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l\). In other words, if
\(E_{ijkl}Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l > 0\), (resp. \(<0\)) then the bifurcated branch
exists above (resp. below) the critical load \(Œª‚ÇÄ\) only.

\begin{remark}
  I can't prove that the bifurcation equation~\eqref{eq:20220801092222} has at
  most \((3^m - 1) / 2\) pairs of real solutions.
\end{remark}

Turning now to the eigenpairs of the Hessian of the energy along
the bifurcated branch, Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220609133608} shows that \(Œ±‚ÇÅ = 0\). Then
\(Œ± = Œ±‚ÇÇ Œ∑¬≤ / 2 + o(Œ∑¬≤)\) and, from Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220616082923}
\begin{equation}
  \bigl(E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l + Œª‚ÇÇ F_{ij} \bigr) œá‚ÇÄ^j = Œ±‚ÇÇ œá‚ÇÄ^i.
\end{equation}

If \((E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l + Œª‚ÇÇ F_{ij} )\) is positive definite, then the
bifurcated branch is stable (note that, in that case, the bifurcated branch
exists above the critical load only). If one of the eigenvalues of this tensor
is \(<0\), then the bifurcated branch is unstable. The stability is undecided
when all eigenvalues are \(‚â• 0\).

\begin{remark}
  Note that, from Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220801092222},
  \begin{equation}
    E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l + Œª‚ÇÇ F_{ij} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j = \tfrac{2}{3} E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l
  \end{equation}

\end{remark}

To conclude this section, it is observed that, when \(Œª‚ÇÅ = 0\), the dominant
term of the potential energy along the bifurcated branch is of the fourth order
[see Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220525053600}]. Combining with Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220801093236},
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220801094437}
  \begin{aligned}[b]
    ‚Ñ∞[u(Œ∑), Œª(Œ∑)]
    &= ‚Ñ∞\{u^{\ast}[Œª(Œ∑)], Œª(Œ∑)\} + \tfrac{1}{24} Œ∑‚Å¥ \bigl(E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l  + 6  Œª‚ÇÇ F_{ij} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j\bigr) + o(Œ∑‚Å¥)\\
    &= ‚Ñ∞\{u^{\ast}[Œª(Œ∑)], Œª(Œ∑)\} - \tfrac{1}{24} Œ∑‚Å¥ E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l + o(Œ∑‚Å¥).
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The expansion \(Œª = Œª‚ÇÄ + Œª‚ÇÇ Œ∑¬≤ / 2 + o(Œ∑¬≤)\) can be inverted as follows
\begin{equation}
  Œ∑‚Å¥ = \frac{4 \bigl(Œª - Œª‚ÇÄ\bigr)¬≤}{Œª‚ÇÇ¬≤} + o(Œª¬≤) = \frac{36 \bigl( F_{ij} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j \bigr)¬≤}{\bigl( E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l \bigr)¬≤} \bigl( Œª - Œª‚ÇÄ \bigr)¬≤
\end{equation}
and expression~\eqref{eq:20220801094437} reads
\begin{equation}
  ‚Ñ∞[u(Œ∑), Œª(Œ∑)] = ‚Ñ∞\{u^{\ast}[Œª(Œ∑)], Œª(Œ∑)\} - \frac{3 \bigl( F_{ij} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j \bigr)¬≤}{2 E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l } \bigl( Œª - Œª‚ÇÄ \bigr)¬≤  + o(Œª¬≤).
\end{equation}

Again, the above expression does not depend on the scaling of \(u‚ÇÅ\) (of the
\(Œæ‚ÇÅ^i\)). Note that, if \(E_{ijkl} Œæ‚ÇÅ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k Œæ‚ÇÅ^l > 0\), then only loads
that are greater than the critical load can be reached on the bifurcated branch,
where the energy is lower than the fundamental branch.

\medskip

The above discussion simplifies considerably when there is only one buckling
mode (\(m = 1\)). This is addressed in the next section.

\section{The case of a single mode}

In this section, we discuss the case \(m = 1\); all tensors considered above
(\(F_{ij}\), \(E_{ijk}\), \(E_{ijkl}\)) then reduce to simple scalars. To avoid
ambiguity, indices are kept: \(F_{11}\), \(E_{11}\), \(E_{11}\). Since
\(\dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ)\) is negative definite over \(V\), we have \(F_{11} < 0\).

It is first observed that the following conditions are \emph{necessary} to
ensure stability of the critical point
\begin{equation}
  E_{111} = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad E_{1111} ‚â• 0,
\end{equation}
which shows that \emph{asymmetric} bifurcation points are always
\emph{unstable}.

\subsection{Asymmetric bifurcations}

We first consider the case \(E_{111} ‚â† 0\). Owing to the discussion above, the
bifurcation point is unstable. Setting \(Œª_1=1\), Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220524135036} delivers
\begin{equation}
  E_{111} Œæ_1^1 + 2F_{11} = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad u(Œª) = u^\ast(Œª) - \frac{2F_{11}}{E_{111}} \bigl( Œª - Œª_0 \bigr) v_1 + o(Œª - Œª_0).
\end{equation}

Furthermore, the hessian of the energy along the bifurcated branch is retrieved
from Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220819160235}
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}[b]
    ‚Ñ∞_{, uu}[u(Œ∑), Œª(Œ∑), v_1, v_1] &= Œ∑ \bigl(E_{111} Œæ_1^1 + Œª‚ÇÅ F_{11}\bigr) + o(Œ∑) = -2 Œ∑ F_{11} + o(Œ∑)\\
    &= -2 F_{11} \bigl( Œª - Œª‚ÇÄ \bigr) + o(Œª - Œª‚ÇÄ).
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Asymmetric bifurcations branches are \emph{unstable} for \(Œª ‚â§ Œª_0\) and
\emph{stable} for \(Œª > Œª‚ÇÄ\) (stability switch).

\subsection{Symmetric bifurcations}

We now consider the case \(E_{111}=0\). From the general discussion of

  + \tfrac{1}{2} Œ∑¬≤ \bigl[‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(x‚ÇÇ, \hat{u}) + 2‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(u‚ÇÅ, x‚ÇÅ, \hat{u}) + 2 Œª‚ÇÅ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(x‚ÇÅ, \hat{u}) + ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÑ(u‚ÇÅ, u‚ÇÅ, x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u})\\
  + ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(u‚ÇÇ, x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u}) + 2Œª‚ÇÅ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÉ(u‚ÇÅ, x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u}) + Œª‚ÇÅ¬≤ \ddot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u}) + Œª‚ÇÇ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u}) \bigr] + o(Œ∑¬≤)
\end{multline*}
(where the \(‚Ñ∞_k\) and \(\dot{‚Ñ∞}_k\) are all evaluated at \(Œª=Œª‚ÇÄ\)) and into the
scalar product \(Œ± ‚å© x, \hat{u} ‚å™\) on the other hand
\begin{multline*}
    Œ± ‚å© x, \hat{u} ‚å™ = Œ±‚ÇÄ ‚å© x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u} ‚å™ + Œ∑ \bigl(Œ±‚ÇÅ ‚å© x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u} ‚å™ + Œ±‚ÇÄ ‚å© x‚ÇÅ, \hat{u} ‚å™\bigr)\\
    + \tfrac{1}{2} Œ∑¬≤ \bigl(Œ±‚ÇÄ ‚å© x‚ÇÇ, \hat{u} ‚å™ + 2 Œ±‚ÇÅ ‚å© x‚ÇÅ, \hat{u} ‚å™ + Œ±‚ÇÇ ‚å© x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u} ‚å™\bigr) + o(Œ∑¬≤).
\end{multline*}

Equating both expressions for all \(\hat{u} ‚àà U\) [see
Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220617074949}] leads to three variational problems (for the
\(Œ∑‚Å∞\), \(Œ∑¬π\) and \(Œ∑¬≤\) terms) that are discussed below.

\paragraph{Variational problem of order 0} Find \(x‚ÇÄ‚ààU\) and \(Œ±‚ÇÄ‚àà\reals\) such
that, for all \(\hat{u}‚ààU\)
\begin{equation*}
  ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u}) = Œ±‚ÇÄ ‚å© x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u} ‚å™.
\end{equation*}

The above equation shows that \((Œ±‚ÇÄ, x‚ÇÄ)\) is an eigenpair of \(‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ)\). As
discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:20220617075558}, only the case \(Œ±‚ÇÄ = 0\) is
relevant. Then \(x‚ÇÄ ‚àà V\), which is expressed by the
expansion~\eqref{eq:20220609133608} of \(x‚ÇÄ\).

\paragraph{Variational problem of order 1} Find \(x‚ÇÅ‚ààU\) and \(Œ±‚ÇÅ‚àà\reals\) such
that, for all \(\hat{u}‚ààU\)
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220609131953}
  ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(x‚ÇÅ, \hat{u}) + ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(u‚ÇÅ, x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u}) + Œª‚ÇÅ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u}) = Œ±‚ÇÅ ‚å© x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u} ‚å™,
\end{equation}
or, equivalently, plugging the expansions~\eqref{eq:20220524133944} and
\eqref{eq:20220609133608} of \(u‚ÇÅ\) and \(x‚ÇÄ\) in the \(v_i\) basis
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:20220617080547}
  ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(x‚ÇÅ, \hat{u}) + ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(v_j, v_k, \hat{u}) œá‚ÇÄ^j Œæ‚ÇÅ^k + Œª‚ÇÅ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(v_j, \hat{u}) œá‚ÇÄ^j = Œ±‚ÇÅ œá‚ÇÄ^j ‚å© v_j, \hat{u} ‚å™.
\end{equation}

For \(\hat{u} = v_i\), observing that \(‚å© v_i, v_j ‚å™ = Œ¥_{ij}\) since
\((v_i)\) is orthonormal, the above equation reads
\begin{equation}
  \bigl[‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v_i, v_j, v_k) Œæ‚ÇÅ^k + Œª‚ÇÅ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(Œª‚ÇÄ; v_i, v_j)\bigr] œá‚ÇÄ^j = Œ±‚ÇÅ œá‚ÇÄ^i,
\end{equation}
which reduces to Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220609133608}.

The test function is now picked in \(W = V^\perp\), and \(x‚ÇÅ\) is decomposed as
the sum of its projections onto \(V\) and \(W\): \(x‚ÇÅ = œá‚ÇÅ^i v_i + y‚ÇÅ\), where
\(y‚ÇÅ ‚àà W\). Eq.~\eqref{eq:20220617080547} then delivers the following
variational problem: find \(y‚ÇÅ ‚àà W\) such that, for all \(\hat{w} ‚àà W\),
\begin{equation}
  ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÇ(y‚ÇÅ, \hat{w}) + ‚Ñ∞‚ÇÉ(v_i, v_j, \hat{w}) œá‚ÇÄ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j + Œª‚ÇÅ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(v_i, \hat{w}) œá‚ÇÄ^i = 0,
\end{equation}
(observe that \(‚å© v_j, \hat{w} ‚å™\) since \(V\) and \(W\) are orthogonal
subspaces). The solution to the above problem is expressed as a linear
combination of the \(w_i\) and \(w_{ij}\) defined by the variational problems
\eqref{eq:20220524134525} and \eqref{eq:20220519164523}, respectively:
\(y‚ÇÅ = œá‚ÇÄ^i Œæ‚ÇÅ^j w_{i j} + \tfrac{1}{2} Œª‚ÇÅ œá‚ÇÄ^i w_i\), and the
decomposition~\eqref{eq:20220609133629} is retrieved.

  + 2Œª‚ÇÅ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÉ(u‚ÇÅ, x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u}) + Œª‚ÇÅ¬≤ \ddot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u}) + Œª‚ÇÇ \dot{‚Ñ∞}‚ÇÇ(x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u}) = 2 Œ±‚ÇÅ ‚å© x‚ÇÅ, \hat{u} ‚å™ + Œ±‚ÇÇ ‚å© x‚ÇÄ, \hat{u} ‚å™.
